Human-Computer Interaction

Master the principles and theories of designing user-friendly interactive systems

What is Human-Computer Interaction?

Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) is a multidisciplinary field of study that focuses on the design, evaluation, and implementation of interactive computing systems for human use.

Essentially, it is about making technology more user-friendly, efficient, and enjoyable.

Key Point

HCI brings together knowledge from computer science, psychology, design, and other fields to create better user experiences.

Why HCI Matters

Increased Productivity

Well-designed interfaces streamline workflows and improve efficiency

Enhanced Satisfaction

Positive user experiences lead to greater adoption and engagement

Improved Accessibility

HCI principles ensure technology is accessible to all users

Reduced Errors

Careful design minimizes user errors in critical applications

Key Disciplines Involved

Computer Science

Provides the technical foundation for building interactive systems

Psychology

Offers insights into human cognition, perception, and behavior

Design

Focuses on creating visually appealing and intuitive interfaces

Ergonomics

Addresses the physical aspects of human-computer interaction

Sociology

Examines the social impact of technology

Core Focus Areas of HCI

Usability

Focuses on how well users can learn and use a system to accomplish their tasks. It emphasizes efficiency, effectiveness, and user satisfaction.

Key Aspects:
  • Ease of learning: How quickly can users become proficient?
  • Efficiency: How quickly can users perform tasks?
  • Memorability: How easily can users remember how to use the system?
  • Errors: How many errors do users make, and how severe are they?

User Experience (UX)

Emphasizes how a system caters to the user's feelings, perceptions, and responses before, during, and after their interaction to promote satisfaction and enjoyment.

Key Aspects:
  • Emotional response: Is the experience enjoyable, frustrating, or satisfying?
  • Aesthetics: Is the interface visually appealing?
  • Value: Does the system meet the user's needs and expectations?

Interaction Design

Focuses on how users input commands and receive feedback to and from the system.

Key Aspects:
  • Input and output methods: Choosing appropriate ways for users to interact (touch, voice, gesture)
  • Interface design: Creating intuitive and effective interfaces
  • Information architecture: Organizing information logically

Accessibility

Focuses on developing systems that cater to people with diverse needs and abilities.

Key Aspects:
  • Designing for diverse needs: Considering visual, auditory, motor, and cognitive impairments
  • Adhering to standards: Following guidelines like WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)

Social Computing

Studies and designs technologies that support and mediate social interactions.

Key Aspects:
  • Social media design: Creating platforms for online communities
  • Collaborative tools: Designing systems that enable teamwork
  • Online communication: Studying virtual interactions

Emerging Technologies

Novel and rapidly developing technologies transforming human-computer interaction.

Key Aspects:
  • VR/AR: Virtual and Augmented Reality
  • AI and Machine Learning: Intelligent systems
  • IoT: Internet of Things
  • Wearable Computing: Smart devices

Lesson 2: Components of HCI

The Human Component

Humans are the users of computers. HCI should always consider what users expect and need, their physical abilities and limitations, how their perceptual systems work, and what they find attractive and enjoyable.

Key Aspects of "Human" in HCI:
Cognitive Abilities

How humans perceive, process, and remember information including attention, memory, problem-solving, and decision-making

Perceptual Capabilities

How humans sense the world through sight, hearing, touch, and other senses

Physical Characteristics

Human anatomy, physiology, and motor skills that affect interaction comfort and safety

Emotional Factors

User feelings and attitudes that influence interactions and experiences

Social & Cultural Context

How cultural values, beliefs, and behaviors shape technology adoption

Individual Differences

Diverse needs, abilities, and preferences requiring accessible and inclusive design

The Computer Component

Computer refers to any interactive computing system that a human user interacts with:

  • Traditional computers (desktops, laptops)
  • Mobile devices (smartphones, tablets)
  • Wearable devices (smartwatches, VR headsets)
  • Embedded systems (devices in cars, appliances)
  • Interactive kiosks and public displays
Key Aspects of "Computer" in HCI:
Input Devices

Allow users to provide input: keyboards, mouse, touchscreens, microphones, cameras, sensors

Output Devices

Present information to users: monitors, speakers, printers, haptic devices

Software

Interface through which users interact: operating systems, applications, user interfaces

Hardware

Physical components of the computer system

The Interaction Component

Interaction refers to the communication and exchange of information between a human user and a computer system.

Key Aspects of Interaction:
1
Interaction Styles

The way a user communicates with a computer system - the method by which users provide input and receive output

2
User Interface (UI)

The visual and interactive elements of a system that users interact with

3
User Experience (UX)

The overall feeling and satisfaction users have when interacting with a system

4
Information Architecture

How information is organized and structured within an interactive system

The Development Process

The Development Process in HCI is a user-centered approach to designing interactive systems. It focuses on understanding users, their needs, and how they interact with technology.

1
User-Centered Design (UCD)

A design philosophy that prioritizes the user's needs and involves users throughout the design process

2
Usability Testing

Evaluating system usability by observing users as they interact with it

3
Prototyping

Creating early versions of a system to test and refine design concepts

4
Iterative Design

Repeated cycles of design, prototyping, testing, and refinement

The Context

Context refers to the dynamic setting in which a user interacts with a computer system.

Task Context

The specific jobs users are trying to accomplish

Environmental Context

The physical environment where interaction occurs (noisy office, mobile setting, etc.)

Social Context

Social interactions during system use (collaboration, communication)

Organizational Context

The organizational structure and culture where the system is used

Interaction Styles

Interaction styles describe the way a user communicates with a computer system - the method by which users provide input and receive output.

Command-Line Interfaces (CLI)

Text-based interaction where programs display instructions and results as text, and users interact by typing commands via keyboard.

Graphical User Interfaces (GUI)

Allows users to interact through graphic elements such as icons, buttons, text boxes, and menus.

Voice User Interfaces (VUI)

Enables users to communicate with the system using voice or speech instructions. Examples: Siri, Alexa, chatbots.

Natural User Interfaces (NUI)

Allows users to control systems by intuitive actions linked to everyday human behavior, such as touch, gestures, and body movements.

Tangible User Interfaces (TUI)

Users interact with digital systems by manipulating real physical objects connected to the system.

Lesson 3: HCI Principles

Design principles guide the creation of usable and effective interactive systems.

The fundamental principles of HCI include User-Centeredness, Visibility and Feedback, Consistency and Standards, Error Prevention and Recovery, User Control and Freedom, Match Between System and Real World, Flexibility and Efficiency, Aesthetic Design, Recognition Rather than Recall, and Help and Documentation.

Visibility and Feedback

A well-designed system should always provide users accurate and prompt information about the system's activity and the status of their actions.

Key Takeaway

Good feedback keeps users informed about what's happening, confirming their actions, reducing uncertainty, and helping them recover from errors.

Lesson 4: HCI Theories

Cognitive Theories

Cognitive theories help us understand how people think, learn, remember, and solve problems when using computers and interactive systems.

Information Processing Model

Compares the human mind to a computer, suggesting information is processed through stages: input, encoding, storage, and retrieval.

Mental Model Theory

People understand and interact with the world by building internal representations based on prior experiences and knowledge.

Cognitive Architecture

Outlines the fundamental structures and processes of the mind, explaining reasoning, problem-solving, and decision-making.

Social Theories

Social theories examine how people interact with technology within social contexts, considering culture, society, and relationships.

Key Social Theories:
  • Social Constructivism - learning shaped by social interactions
  • Actor-Network Theory - complex networks of actors and technologies
  • Activity Theory - understanding activities within context
  • Social Cognitive Theory - learning through observation and social context

Perception Theories

Perception theories explain how humans receive, interpret, and make sense of sensory information from computer interfaces.

Gestalt Psychology

Emphasizes organizing sensory inputs into meaningful wholes using principles like:

  • Similarity - similar elements appear related
  • Proximity - close elements appear grouped
  • Continuation - following visual flow
  • Symmetry - creating balance
  • Figure-Ground - distinguishing foreground from background

Motor Skills Theories

Motor skills theories help us understand how users physically interact with technology.

Fitts's Law

Predicts the time required to move to and select a target based on distance and size. Closer and larger targets are faster to acquire.

Movement Time = f(Distance, Target Size)
Hick's Law

Reaction time increases as the number of choices increases. More options = longer decision time.

Reaction Time ‚àù Number of Choices
Design Implication

Make clickable targets large and close to reduce interaction time (Fitts's Law). Simplify choices to speed up decision-making (Hick's Law).

Summary

Human-Computer Interaction combines principles from multiple disciplines to create user-friendly, efficient, and enjoyable interactive systems.

Understanding users is fundamental to good design
Design principles guide the creation of usable interfaces
Theories provide frameworks for understanding interaction
Iterative design and testing improve user experience
